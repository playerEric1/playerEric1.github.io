expansions of hominins out of Africa

Based on the journal article: Chronologic constraints on hominin dispersal outside Africa since 2.48 Ma, Jordan (Scardia et al., 2019)

In 2019, new discoveries by the team of Scardia et al. gives us a completely new sight into the earliest exit of hominins out of Africa. The archaeological site was the Dawqara Formation in the Zarqa Valley, Jordan. Dawqara Fm has been known since the early 1980s because of the presence of Oldowan tools and mammal fossils at different stratigraphic levels within its fluvial sediments, consisting of choppers, cores, and flakes.

Outline of the research

Our knowledge about the earliest dispersal of hominins out of Africa has been greatly improved with the recent discovery of Oldowan stone tools in North Africa at ca. 2.4 Ma and in China since ca. 2.1 Ma (Sahnouni et al., 2018), providing us with a new image for the earliest migration out of Africa.

However, no Paleolithic sites of such age exist in the Levant corridor, which together with Bab-el-Mandeb strait are the two most important passages out of Africa. So far, the oldest evidence in the Levant may be traced to Aïn al Fil (Syria), where a paleontologically anchored magnetostratigraphic age of ca. 2.0–1.8 Ma has been proposed for Oldowan tools retrieved therefrom (Le Tensorer et al., 2015). This site is older than the broadly accepted ‘Ubeidiya (Israel), which mostly yielded early Acheulian artifacts (Bar-Yosef and Belfer-Cohen, 2001). ‘Ubeidiya has been constrained between 1.5 and 1.2 Ma, based on long-range biochronologic correlations (e.g., Martínez-Navarro et al., 2009)

In this research, new chronologic constraints were found from surveys and dating methods (paleomagnetic, 40Ar/39Ar, and U-Pb dating methods). They showed that the Dawqara Formation was deposited between about 2.52 Ma and the Matuyama–Olduvai geomagnetic reversal (1.95 Ma). By linear interpolation, the artifact-bearing stratigraphic levels within the Dawqara Formation have ages from about 2.5 Ma to 2.0 Ma. This marks the earliest hominin dispersal out of Africa, pre-dating by ca. 300 kyr the hominin occupation of Chinese Loess Plateau.

Techniques

Test excavations were conducted in 2014 and the formal one started in 2015. The archaeological investigations were mostly vertical exploration of the exposed surface of each outcrop and by positioning each artifact with the aid of the total station. Modern agricultural activities had produced wide exposures of the Dawqara Fm, which brought great advantages to the vertical exploration, which is considered the best approach in the research.

How to tell whether a stone is an artifact?

Artifacts in the Dawqara Fm were distinguished from natural stones according to the following criteria:

  1. if flaked pieces (cores and choppers):
    1. a minimum of two bulb-scars >1 cm in which the angle between the scar and the striking platform is  <90°;
    2. evident concavity of the scars, which means a prominent bulb on the corresponding flake;
  2. if detached pieces (flakes, retouched or not):
    1. clear bulb with evident point of impact;
    2. angle between butt and bulbar face  >90°;

The occurrence of a recurring pattern of unidirectional débitage on cores was attributed to intentional human trimming.

Results


The artifacts all came from several locations within a sequence of fluvial terraces in an area of ca. 30 km2. Choppers, cores, and flakes were found within the fluvial gravel of the Dawqara Fm, forming the highest fluvial terrace of the Zarqa Valley; handaxes, cores and flakes were found in a lower terrace (Birayn Fm), indicating that there is a broad agreement between morpho-stratigraphic succession, i.e. Oldowan artifact in the older (high terrace) Dawqara Fm and Acheulian tools in the younger (lower terrace) Birayn Fm. The authors’ team excavated a 30-meter high fluvial terrace, a stepped geological formation carved out by a river. The water source would have supported a variety of local wildlife, and, potentially, humans.

The authors estimated by linear interpolation individual ages for the stratigraphic layers of 2.48 Ma (334-lower), 2.24 Ma (334-upper), 2.16 Ma (331), 2.06 Ma (330), and 1.95 Ma (332), respectively (See green bars in Fig. 11). No evidence of artifacts in the Dulayl Fm (i.e. before 2.52 Ma) was found.

The Dawqara stone tools are clearly characterized by features considered as a benchmark in human technological evolution. The recurrence of flaking patterns, such as the unidirectional flaking, indicates intentionality in tool-production, eliminating not only gravity and other natural causes, but also the casual production of unintentionally trimmed flakes and pounding stones. The Dawqara toolkit at 334-lower shows technical features such as recurrent unipolar flaking.

No conclusion was made about the makers of stone tools in the Dawqara Fm. As shown in Fig. 1, three species of hominins (early Homo sapiens, Homo rudolfensis, Homo habilis) are coincident with the Dawqara Fm. We cannot tell the differences between the techniques of stone tool making of different species. The only robust evidence is their fossil, which needs further exploration.

Conclusions

The authors’ team integrated three independent dating methods to build a robust age model and found the earliest hominin activity in the Zarqa Valley to be ca. 2.48 Ma. The new stratigraphical discovery in the Zarqa Valley, Jordan, firmly shows hominins’ presence in the Levant at the beginning of Pleistocene. It fills a temporal gap, providing new archeological evidence older than ‘Ubeidiya and consistent with Aïn al Fil at ca. 2 Ma in Syria, agrees which evidence from North Africa at ca. 2.4 Ma and China at ca. 2.1 Ma.

Expansion of Hominins at the beginning of Pleistocene fits into a broader context of climate changes and savannah expansion (Potts, 2012), which could have had a role in triggering or making possible the hominin occupation of Asia.

The research also shades some insights about the technical and adaptive skills of the early hominins. They entered the Levant at the beginning of Pleistocene with the ability to perform repeated stone working by hard-hammer percussion and they were apparently able to recognize and select local chert cobbles as convenient raw material, preferring it to the locally available basalt. This choice could reflect a precise skill in iteratively observing and assessing the environmental resource, which is a remarkable feature in sustaining hominin survival.
